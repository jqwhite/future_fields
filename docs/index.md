    <h2 class="c18 c15" id="h.k3prgjdn8hos">
        <span class="c27 c9">Abstract</span>
    </h2>
    <p class="c33 c15">
        <span class="c0">The Future Fields project evaluates crop suitability under current and mid-century climate scenarios in the U.S. Southwest, focusing on Arizona, New Mexico, Utah, and Colorado. By analyzing 618,694 fields from the USDA Crop Sequence Boundary dataset (2016-2023) and 2,580 crops from the FAO EcoCrop database, the project assesses the impact of temperature, sunlight, rainfall, USDA hardiness zones, K&ouml;ppen-Geiger climate zones, and soil pH on crop viability. The analysis reveals discrepancies between current agricultural practices and the crops deemed most suitable for future climates, suggesting that factors such as economic considerations, land use restrictions, and irrigation practices strongly influence crop selection, potentially at the expense of optimizing crop suitability under changing conditions. The study also highlights the resilience of crop suitability across mid-century scenarios, indicating that adaptation strategies based on climate-dependent factors could be robust to varying levels of climate change. While the project provides valuable insights, it underscores the need for further research into the role of irrigation, land use changes, and economic factors in future crop viability. The findings serve as a foundation for refining crop suitability models and developing more comprehensive strategies for agricultural adaptation in response to climate change.</span>
    </p>
    <h2 class="c18 c15" id="h.pmtwvjy72iuf">
        <span class="c27 c9">Introduction</span>
    </h2>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>The most reliable predictor of what crop will be planted in a particular field for a growing season (the crop profile for the field) is what crops were planted in previous seasons. &nbsp;However, climate change will force crop profiles into regimes outside of the historical context </span>
        <span class="c13 c9">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/BS8Lp%2BOghmd&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523530580&amp;usg=AOvVaw2qhMJJZeV3m1HbPgXnYjzU">[1,2]</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c0">. The goal of this project is to assess which crops are suitable for fields in the Southwest United States given the growth requirements of the crop and a set of climate-dependent conditions for the field: weather, water, sun, and soil.</span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>Specifically, Future Fields assesses the suitability of crops for fields in four states of the US Southwest for medium carbon and high carbon mid-century (2039 to 2065) climate scenarios. Each field in the </span>
        <span class="c40">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.nass.usda.gov/Research_and_Science/Crop-Sequence-Boundaries/&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523531005&amp;usg=AOvVaw3gd_P_i87Co6is5y4zQTu5">Crop Sequence Boundary</a>
        </span>
        <span>&nbsp;2016-2023 dataset for Arizona, New Mexico, Utah, and Colorado (618,694 fields) was assessed for each crop in the </span>
        <span class="c40">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://gaez.fao.org/pages/ecocrop&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523531185&amp;usg=AOvVaw1mCXEJHUW_SGRYnYfivG6z">FAO EcoCrop database</a>
        </span>
        <span>&nbsp;(2,580 species)</span>
        <span>. Scores were calculated for each field, crop combination based on climate-dependent features that have been modeled into the future: &nbsp;temperature, sunlight during the growth season, rainfall, USDA plant hardiness zone, and K&ouml;ppen-Geiger global climate zone. In addition, soil pH was selected to represent climate-independent soil characteristics of the field </span>
        <span class="c13 c9">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/mCF7&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523531391&amp;usg=AOvVaw0RFkVMZsnJLuj02veOCMp_">[3]</a>
        </span>
        <span>. </span>
        <span class="c0">The six features were combined into an overall score to assess the suitability of the crop for the field given a climate scenario.</span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>Previous studies have focused on the impacts of climate change on a few, economically important crops </span>
        <span class="c13 c9">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/6OsW%2B7b1m&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523531668&amp;usg=AOvVaw2Jv9PgheHU56fin7nLy6dH">[4,5]</a>
        </span>
        <span>, or estimating the geographic changes in suitability for a single crop </span>
        <span class="c13 c9">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/GgeS&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523531829&amp;usg=AOvVaw2D3ETfSmO2X7rBTJoxpNJj">[6]</a>
        </span>
        <span>&nbsp;or a small subset of geographically selected crops </span>
        <span class="c13 c9">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/3sjl&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523531985&amp;usg=AOvVaw30bcvtck8pNxrmDxkgLkYo">[7]</a>
        </span>
        <span>.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;More comprehensive studies have focused on the regional or global suitability of a selected set of food, feed, fiber, and energy crops </span>
        <span class="c13 c9">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/GLAV%2B6b8a&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523532165&amp;usg=AOvVaw3-IfCAAVOLi0AxGxfO7k0u">[8,9]</a>
        </span>
        <span>. &nbsp;The emphasis of available studies seems to be evaluating the response of selected distributions to climate change, rather than discovery of alternative crops. </span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>Thus, the goals of the Future Fields Project are not only to assess the suitability of existing crop profiles for future climate scenarios, but also to enable discovery of new crop profiles to match the new climate reality of agricultural fields in the USA. Current climate and soil conditions are useful tools for crop selection </span>
        <span class="c13 c9">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/B3cf&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523532451&amp;usg=AOvVaw13p5aaQvVOH3bckoTjs2fz">[10]</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c0">, so it is logical to extend suitability analysis to future climate scenarios. &nbsp;Overall, Future Fields reveals new agricultural patterns forced by global warming.</span>
    </p>
    <h2 class="c18 c15" id="h.fmquvvgc1ikj">
        <span class="c9 c27">Methods</span>
    </h2>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>Datasets, sources, and methods are detailed in the </span>
        <span class="c40">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://github.com/jqwhite/future_fields/&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523532801&amp;usg=AOvVaw0jCsGGT_O5VcXMRLq0AC6B">Future Fields GitHub repository.</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c0">&nbsp;</span>
    </p>
    <h3 class="c29 c15" id="h.oiu3d5k5fwf9">
        <span>Dataset rationale and </span>
        <span class="c14">selection</span>
    </h3>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c28">The overall goal of the project was to assess current and potential future crop growth conditions in order to provide insights for agricultural decision-making in the face of climate change. Thus, the source datasets for the project </span>
        <span>represent</span>
        <span class="c28">
            &nbsp;fields in the US, a wide variety of crops, and two climate scenarios.
            <br>
            <br>
            For 
        </span>
        <span>fields</span>
        <span class="c28">, the USDA Crop Sequence Boundary </span>
        <span>(CSB) </span>
        <span class="c28">dataset was selected because it is a curated, authoritative representation of fields in the USA </span>
        <span class="c13 c9 c50">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/DFr1&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523533357&amp;usg=AOvVaw2VsmrD0jy4UzdKxD19XxCA">[11]</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c0">. &nbsp;It contains the location of the field, the field boundary defined from satellite imagery, validated crop sequences for the past eight years, and various identifiers, for example state, county, and crop codes. </span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c28">For crops, &nbsp;the EcoCrop dataset was selected because it represents a comprehensive, </span>
        <span>curated</span>
        <span class="c28">&nbsp;catalog of crop characteristics </span>
        <span class="c13 c9 c50">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/QcDw&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523533640&amp;usg=AOvVaw3FlLF7zNdP76xdpCMVCVBK">[12]</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c28">. &nbsp;The </span>
        <span class="c40 c50">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://github.com/jqwhite/future_fields/blob/main/data/crops/EcoCrop_Complete.csv&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523533830&amp;usg=AOvVaw12eraEUQuVE5cCyLlCjZoN">Future Fields version</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c28">&nbsp;is a combined version of two variants available on GitHub </span>
        <span class="c13 c9 c50">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/6b8a%2Bfjsr&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523533997&amp;usg=AOvVaw177hiNIvT7BOtuFr2GgQ6v">[9,13]</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c28">. &nbsp;Missing values were imputed from crops within the same genus or the </span>
        <span>mean </span>
        <span class="c28">of a set of crops clustered by similarity (</span>
        <span>agglomerative clustering)</span>
        <span class="c28">. </span>
        <span class="c0">&nbsp;</span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c28">For climate projections, </span>
        <span>datasets</span>
        <span class="c28">&nbsp;</span>
        <span class="c0">were selected to represent two scenarios: moderate mitigation and continued high emissions with limited mitigation. &nbsp;Mid-century projections were selected to provide a reasonable view of the near-term impacts of global warming on agricultural outcomes. Projections into the recent past provided a baseline and a basis for comparison to the actual crop sequences. Recent and mid-century climate scenarios were represented by </span>
    </p>
    <ol class="c48 lst-kix_fb2xfw5dps6t-0 start" start="1">
        <li class="c12 c19 li-bullet-0">
            <span>Localized Constructed Analogs (LOCA) datasets derived from the Fourth National Climate Assessment Representative Concentration Pathway 4.5 (RCP4.5; medium-carbon) or RCP 8.5 (high-carbon) projections </span>
            <span class="c13 c9">
                <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/k6pg%2B2vSQ%2BfWbA&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523534559&amp;usg=AOvVaw348crQe6ZGHlO7POVPPx5K">[14&ndash;16]</a>
            </span>
            <span class="c0">, (growing season data).</span>
        </li>
        <li class="c12 c19 li-bullet-0">
            <span>Multivariate Adaptive Constructed Analogs (MACA) datasets based on a common set of 20 Coupled Model Intercomparison Project 5 (CMIP5) global climate models </span>
            <span class="c9 c13">
                <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/7jOn%2ByH5i&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523534819&amp;usg=AOvVaw2wIP-HrWxaQ7KCHDl8VlXd">[17,18]</a>
            </span>
            <span>&nbsp;available from the </span>
            <span class="c40">
                <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://climatetoolbox.org/tool/Climate-Mapper&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523534994&amp;usg=AOvVaw1kju6UR49Dn-pN3ikV0DXg">ClimateToolbox Climate Mapper</a>
            </span>
            <span>&nbsp;</span>
            <span class="c13 c9">
                <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/UZ8g&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523535187&amp;usg=AOvVaw20K5tTQuYkhI2uAKeehKLi">[19]</a>
            </span>
            <span class="c0">&nbsp;(rainfall, temperature, and USDA plant hardiness zone data).</span>
        </li>
        <li class="c12 c19 li-bullet-0">
            <span>Downscaled and bias-corrected climate projections from the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project phase 6 (CMIP6) </span>
            <span class="c13 c9">
                <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/xbwr&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523535438&amp;usg=AOvVaw04uyaXvP9QIHTyXkc9a3g1">[20]</a>
            </span>
            <span>&nbsp; that take into account seven shared Socio-economic Pathways (SSPs) </span>
            <span class="c13 c9">
                <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/73zn&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523535601&amp;usg=AOvVaw0Re7fDAc5D9iuLXzpdl9Wh">[21]</a>
            </span>
            <span>&nbsp;(K&ouml;ppen-Geiger climate zone projections for 2041-2070, datasets for ssp245 and ssp585 </span>
            <span class="c13 c9">
                <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/rxtO&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523535768&amp;usg=AOvVaw3dQuzk8lioeg2D7brR-_E_">[22]</a>
            </span>
            <span>, available from </span>
            <span class="c40">
                <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=http://gloh2o.org&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523535924&amp;usg=AOvVaw1ZOu4fogCaCtIU-aynV5KG">gloh2o.org</a>
            </span>
            <span>&nbsp;</span>
            <span class="c13 c9">
                <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/LAlW&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523536098&amp;usg=AOvVaw3-q-caNvjqjHp8EQGNc3yJ">[23]</a>
            </span>
            <span>).</span>
        </li>
    </ol>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>In summary, the Future Fields mid-century medium-carbon scenarios were represented by datasets derived from RCP 4.5 and ssp245, and mid-century high-carbon scenarios were represented by datasets derived from RCP 8.5 and ssp585. &nbsp;The Future Fields recent scenarios were derived from the LOCA (1976-2005) or MACA (1979-2012) historical models, and from high-resolution, observation-based climatologies from 1991&ndash;2020 for the K&ouml;ppen-Geiger climate zones. &nbsp;</span>
    </p>
    <h3 class="c29 c15" id="h.d3o4w24alg85">
        <span>Suitability scores</span>
    </h3>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c0">Each field was evaluated for each crop. &nbsp;For each scenario (recent, mid-century medium-carbon, and mid-century high-carbon) and field, crop combination, a score was calculated based on field characteristics and crop requirements. &nbsp;The requirements encompassed a set of six critical environmental factors: soil pH, photoperiod, climate zone, temperature, rainfall, and hardiness. </span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>These factors were chosen because they represent critical environmental influences on crop performance (growth and yield), and because they have been modeled for future climate scenarios. &nbsp;For example, temperature and rainfall determine the growing season and water availability. &nbsp;Soil pH is the exception: There is no available soil forecast for mid-century, and it is unclear how soil pH will change with climate </span>
        <span class="c13 c9">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/mCF7&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523536757&amp;usg=AOvVaw2x1Tut1q3xf1F1pcbTbk9U">[3]</a>
        </span>
        <span>, </span>
        <span>so the current pH values from the Gridded National Soil Survey Geographic Database (gNATSGO) were used for all scenarios </span>
        <span class="c13 c9">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://paperpile.com/c/lEs9Kr/GIj9&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523536970&amp;usg=AOvVaw0MKJXexpAOoCm4viKAcYtb">[24]</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c0">. &nbsp;The intent behind selection of these factors is that the suitability scores should reflect the key drivers of crop success in a climate-dependent manner. </span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c0">The scores were combined into a final score matrix, and the best-scoring crops are categorized as suitable crops for that field for that climate scenario.</span>
    </p>
    <h3 class="c29 c15" id="h.msoqldkizu9f">
        <span class="c9 c24">Scoring actual 2016-2023 crop sequences</span>
    </h3>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>Crop sequence boundary data standard crop codes (e.g. 12 &ldquo;Corn&rdquo;, 224 &ldquo;Strawberries&rdquo;) were mapped to EcoCrop crop species using typical crop species content of the CSB crop code. &nbsp;For example, CSB cod 228: &#39;Dbl Crop Triticale/Corn&#39; was assigned to &nbsp;&quot;Likely_Crop_Species&quot;: &#39;[&times; Triticosecale&#39;, &#39;Zea mays&#39;], with the reasoning: &quot;When a field has &#39;Dbl Crop Triticale/Corn&#39; growing in it, the species are most likely &times; Triticosecale (triticale) and Zea mays (corn). Double cropping involves growing two different crops in the same field in one year, usually triticale followed by corn&quot;. &nbsp;The </span>
        <span class="c40">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://github.com/jqwhite/future_fields/blob/main/data/crops/cdl_to_ecocrop_code_translator.csv&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523537425&amp;usg=AOvVaw02oKp1JMpGejck7o5K6tCR">mapping information</a>
        </span>
        <span>&nbsp;and </span>
        <span class="c40">
            <a class="c5" href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https://github.com/jqwhite/future_fields/blob/main/notebooks/CDL_to_ecocrop_crop_code_translator.ipynb&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1724807523537598&amp;usg=AOvVaw0HQSmFpPNMSaQPLlGvQQTr">strategy</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c0">&nbsp;is provided in the Future Fields GitHub repository.</span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c0">After mapping to EcoCrop crops, CSB cropsets were scored, and the highest scoring crop for each field, crop, scenario combination was selected for display and evaluation.</span>
    </p>

    <h3 class="c29 c15" id="h.bn78o4jhmjsw">
        <span class="c24 c9">The match between actual crop profiles and suitable cropsets</span>
    </h3>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>Inspection of the actual crop from 2023 and the suitable cropset contents using the popup feature on the map suggests that the suitable cropsets are relevant to the actual crop content of the field. &nbsp;To investigate this relation more thoroughly, the actual crop profile was compared to the suitable cropset for every field and scenario. &nbsp;The actual crop profile takes into account that different crops might be planted in different seasons in the same field. The CSB dataset contains crop sequences from 2016 to 2023. &nbsp;The full set of crops from these seasons was combined into an overall actual crop profile, and these multiseason crop profiles were compared to the suitable cropsets for each scenario (</span>
        <span>
            <a class="c5" href="#fig_actual_compared_to_suitable">Figure&nbsp;5</a>
        </span>
        <span>). Because the actual crop profiles and suitable cropsets were derived differently, Jaccard Similarity was not appropriate, so a simple assessment was made: whether </span>
        <span class="c53">any</span>
        <span class="c0">&nbsp;of the actual crops were contained within the suitable cropset. &nbsp;This analysis demonstrates that, although the content appears relevant on manual inspection, in most cases the suitable cropsets do not contain crops from the actual profiles.</span>
    </p>
    <table class="c38">
        <tr class="c44">

    <h3 class="c15 c29" id="h.w8xa9d98avqf">
        <span class="c24 c9">Score distributions: actual and suitable crop scores </span>
    </h3>
    <p class="c12">
        <span>The differences observed between actual crop scores and the suitable cropset scores, especially for the recent scenario, prompted a more thorough investigation of the score distributions. &nbsp;</span>
        <span>
            <a class="c5" href="#fig_score_distribution_breakdown">Figure&nbsp;7</a>
        </span>
        <span class="c0">&nbsp;displays the relative score distributions. &nbsp;Inspection of the score categories provides an indication of the selectivity of each suitability score. &nbsp;For example, the climate zone scores were predominantly 1 for the suitable crops and evenly split between -1 and +1, showing that the climate zone score was moderately selective. In other words, for all the suitable crops that had a score of 1, the actual scores were evenly split between -1 and 1. </span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c0">These plots hint that the rainfall score might account for the observation that almost none of the actual crops are in the suitable cropsets: for all suitable scores of +1, the actual scores are split between 0 and +0.5, and for the actual scores of +1, there are no rainfall scores above 0. However, re-calculating overall suitability scores without the rainfall category did not obviously improve the overlap between actual and suitable cropsets (not shown), so the difference in overlap is likely more complex. </span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c0">The distributions of the overall scores provide a hint: there were very few actual crops that achieved a maximum overall score of +6, whereas a reasonable number of EcoCrop species achieved the overall maximum score. It may be that expanding the suitable crops to the top 20% of overall scores would improve the overlap between actual and suitable, because the suitable crops could include species with a 5/6 score and allowing for the possibility that the actual crop would make the cut into the suitable cropset. </span>

    <h2 class="c18 c15" id="h.brl8pk2tbz58">
        <span class="c27 c9">Discussion</span>
    </h2>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c0">The Future Fields project embodies a comprehensive evaluation of crop suitability under two near-future climate scenarios in the U.S. Southwest to provide insights into how agriculture might adapt to a changing climate. One unexpected finding is the discrepancy between actual crop sequences from 2016-2023 and the crops deemed most suitable under both current and projected mid-century climate conditions. One explanation for the divergence may be that factors beyond straightforward suitability scores&ndash;such as economic considerations, land use restrictions, and irrigation practices&ndash;strongly influence crop selection. &nbsp;Alternatively, current agricultural practices may simply not be optimized for crop requirements, especially under changing climate conditions. &nbsp;For instance, while actual crops like corn, winter wheat, and alfalfa are widespread, they do not always align with the highest suitability scores, particularly in regions where temperature and photoperiod scores indicate potential mismatches. Finally, the discrepancy could be due to the influence of factors like irrigation, which is not accounted for in the suitability model but supports crop viability in arid and semi-arid regions.</span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c0">The analysis reveals that the mid-century scenarios, regardless of carbon intensity, generate similar patterns of suitable crops. &nbsp;This result indicates that even moderate mitigation efforts may not sufficiently alter the trajectory of climate change&rsquo;s impact on agriculture. More generally, this finding raises concerns about the resilience of agricultural systems and the need for more robust adaptation strategies. &nbsp;However, the similarity between mid-century scenarios suggests that if an alternate crop sequence is selected for cultivation, it will be robust to the more extreme of the two scenarios. &nbsp;That is, crop sequences will most likely need to change to match new climate realities, but if the change is guided by climate-dependent factors, the change will be insensitive to the precise climate outcome mid-century. Crop suitability analysis for long-term future projections may further inform the selection of alternative crops. </span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c0">A key observation from the study is the role of integrated climate scores, such as the K&ouml;ppen-Geiger climate zone and USDA plant hardiness zone, in predicting crop suitability. These scores, which encapsulate a range of environmental factors, provide a more nuanced understanding of crop-environment interactions than individual factors like temperature or rainfall alone. However, the findings also highlight the need for further refinement of these scores to better capture the complex dynamics of crop suitability under future climate scenarios.</span>
    </p>
    <p class="c12">
        <span class="c0">Looking forward, the Future Fields project lays the groundwork for several avenues of further investigation. Currently, Future Fields does not take into consideration land use changes: urbanization, de-urbanization, and land viability changes. It also does not take into account irrigation and changes in water availablility, eithe through groundwater depletion or surface water changes. Integrating these additional variables should provide a more accurate, more realistic assessment of future agricultural potential. Additionally, exploring the economic and logistical factors that influence crop selection, would offer a more comprehensive &nbsp;understanding of how agriculture might adapt to future challenges. Finally, the project&rsquo;s methodology might also be extended to other regions and crops, providing valuable insights for global agricultural adaptation strategies.</span>
    </p>
